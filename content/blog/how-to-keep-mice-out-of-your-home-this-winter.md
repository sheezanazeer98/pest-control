When temperatures drop in Northeastern Pennsylvania, mice start looking for warm shelter — and your home is the perfect target. A single mouse can squeeze through a gap the size of a dime, and once inside, they breed rapidly. One pair of mice can produce up to 60 offspring in a single year.

Here's everything you need to know about keeping mice out this winter.

## Why Mice Are a Serious Problem

Mice aren't just a nuisance — they're a genuine health and safety hazard:

- **Disease transmission:** Mice carry hantavirus, salmonella, and other pathogens through their droppings, urine, and saliva
- **Contamination:** They contaminate food and food preparation surfaces
- **Property damage:** Mice gnaw on wires (fire hazard), insulation, pipes, and structural materials
- **Allergies:** Mouse dander and droppings are significant allergens, especially for children

## Signs You Have Mice

Before you can solve the problem, you need to know it exists. Watch for:

- **Droppings:** Small, dark pellets (about the size of a grain of rice) in cabinets, along walls, and near food sources
- **Gnaw marks:** Small teeth marks on food packaging, wood, or wires
- **Sounds:** Scratching or scurrying noises in walls or ceilings, especially at night
- **Nests:** Shredded paper, fabric, or insulation bundled in hidden areas
- **Tracks:** Dusty surfaces may show tiny footprints or tail drag marks

## 10 Ways to Mouse-Proof Your Home

### 1. Seal All Entry Points

Inspect your home's exterior thoroughly. Common entry points include:

- Gaps around pipes and utility lines
- Cracks in the foundation
- Spaces under doors (install door sweeps)
- Openings around vents and chimneys
- Gaps where the roof meets the walls

Use steel wool and caulk to seal small gaps. Mice can't chew through steel wool.

### 2. Store Food Properly

Keep all food in glass or heavy plastic containers with tight-fitting lids. Don't leave pet food out overnight, and store birdseed and grass seed in sealed containers.

### 3. Maintain a Clean Kitchen

Wipe down counters and sweep floors daily. Clean under appliances regularly, as crumbs accumulate in these hidden areas.

### 4. Manage Trash Effectively

Use trash cans with tight-fitting lids both inside and outside your home. Take garbage out regularly.

### 5. Reduce Clutter

Mice love clutter for nesting material and hiding spots. Keep storage areas organized and use plastic bins instead of cardboard boxes.

### 6. Trim Vegetation Near Your Home

Overgrown shrubs and tree branches near your house provide cover for mice. Keep vegetation trimmed back at least 12 inches from exterior walls.

### 7. Stack Firewood Away From Your Home

Store firewood at least 20 feet from your house and keep it elevated off the ground.

### 8. Address Moisture Issues

Fix leaky pipes and faucets. Mice need water to survive, and eliminating water sources makes your home less attractive.

### 9. Check Your Garage and Attic

These are common entry points and nesting areas. Inspect them regularly and seal any gaps you find.

### 10. Consider Professional Exclusion

A professional pest control company can identify entry points you might miss and implement comprehensive exclusion strategies.
