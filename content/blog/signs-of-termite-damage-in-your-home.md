Termites are called "silent destroyers" for good reason. They can chew through wood, flooring, and even wallpaper undetected for years, causing thousands of dollars in structural damage before you ever notice a problem.

In Northeastern Pennsylvania, subterranean termites are the most common species. They live in underground colonies and build mud tubes to access the wood in your home. Here's what to watch for.

## 1. Mud Tubes on Foundation Walls

Subterranean termites build pencil-thin mud tubes along foundation walls, crawl spaces, and piers. These tubes provide moisture and protection as termites travel between their underground colony and your home's wood.

**Where to look:** Exterior foundation walls, interior basement walls, crawl space piers, and around plumbing penetrations.

## 2. Hollow-Sounding Wood

Termites eat wood from the inside out, leaving a thin veneer of wood (or paint) on the surface. If you tap on wood that sounds hollow or papery, termites may have consumed the interior.

**How to check:** Use the handle of a screwdriver to tap along baseboards, window frames, door frames, and exposed wood beams.

## 3. Discarded Wings Near Windows

When termite colonies mature, they produce "swarmers" — winged reproductive termites that fly out to start new colonies. After swarming, they shed their wings.

**What to look for:** Piles of small, translucent wings near windowsills, light fixtures, or doorways, typically in spring.

## 4. Bubbling or Peeling Paint

When termites tunnel close to the surface of painted wood, moisture from their tunneling can cause paint to bubble, peel, or appear water-damaged.

**Important:** This sign is often mistaken for water damage. If you can't identify a water source, termites may be the cause.

## 5. Frass (Termite Droppings)

Drywood termites (less common in PA but still present) push their droppings out of small holes near their tunnels. These droppings look like tiny, dark pellets or sawdust.

**Where to find it:** On windowsills, along baseboards, and beneath wooden furniture.

## 6. Doors and Windows That Stick

As termites consume wood around door and window frames, the frames can warp, making doors and windows difficult to open or close.

**Note:** Seasonal humidity changes can also cause sticking, but if the problem is persistent and worsening, it warrants an inspection.

## 7. Sagging Floors or Ceilings

In advanced infestations, termite damage can compromise structural integrity, leading to sagging floors, buckling ceilings, or visible maze-like patterns in exposed wood.

**Critical:** If you notice structural changes, call a professional immediately. This indicates severe, long-term termite damage.

## How Much Damage Can Termites Do?

The numbers are staggering:

- Termites cause over **$5 billion** in property damage annually in the U.S.
- The average termite damage repair costs **$3,000–$8,000**
- Most homeowner's insurance policies **do not cover** termite damage
- A single colony can eat approximately **1 pound of wood per day**

## Prevention Is Your Best Defense

Annual termite inspections are the most cost-effective way to protect your home. Professional inspectors know where to look and can detect activity long before visible damage occurs.
