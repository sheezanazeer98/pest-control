Strange noises coming from your attic at night? You're not imagining things. Attics provide warmth, shelter, and safety from predators, making them attractive to various wildlife species — especially during the colder months in Northeastern Pennsylvania.

Here's how to identify what's up there and what to do about it.

## Common Attic Invaders in NEPA

### Raccoons

**Sounds:** Heavy thumping, walking, and vocal sounds (chattering, growling)
**When:** Mostly nighttime activity
**Signs:** Large entry holes (4+ inches), heavy damage to insulation, strong odor from droppings

Raccoons are strong and can tear open soffits, roof vents, and fascia boards to gain entry. Females often seek attics in spring to give birth.

### Squirrels

**Sounds:** Rapid scurrying, scratching, and rolling sounds
**When:** Dawn and dusk (most active in early morning)
**Signs:** Chewed entry holes (2-3 inches), gnawed wires, nesting materials

Gray squirrels are the most common attic invader in our area. They can chew through wood and even aluminum to create entry points.

### Bats

**Sounds:** High-pitched squeaking, fluttering
**When:** Dusk (when they leave) and dawn (when they return)
**Signs:** Tiny entry gaps (3/8 inch is enough), guano (droppings) piles below roost points, oily stains around entry points

Pennsylvania is home to several bat species. While bats are beneficial for insect control, they can carry rabies and their guano poses health risks.

### Mice and Rats

**Sounds:** Light scratching, gnawing
**When:** Nighttime primarily
**Signs:** Tiny droppings, gnaw marks, nesting materials

While technically rodents rather than wildlife, they're extremely common attic invaders.

## What NOT to Do

Before we get to solutions, here are critical mistakes to avoid:

1. **Don't seal entry points while animals are inside.** Trapped animals will cause extensive damage trying to escape and may die in your walls.

2. **Don't use poison.** Animals may die in inaccessible areas, creating terrible odors and attracting insects. Some species are also protected by law.

3. **Don't handle wild animals yourself.** Raccoons, bats, and other wildlife can carry rabies and other diseases. Even seemingly docile animals can bite when cornered.

4. **Don't ignore the problem.** Wildlife damage gets worse over time. Animals will expand their territory, contaminate insulation, chew wires (fire hazard), and attract other pests.

## What TO Do: A Step-by-Step Guide

### Step 1: Identify the Animal

Use the sound and sign descriptions above to determine what you're dealing with. If you're unsure, a professional inspection can identify the species quickly.

### Step 2: Document the Damage

Take photos of any visible damage, droppings, or entry points. This will be helpful for both remediation and any insurance claims.

### Step 3: Call a Professional

Wildlife removal requires specialized knowledge, equipment, and licenses. Professionals will:

- Identify the species and assess the situation
- Develop a humane removal plan
- Remove the animals safely
- Seal all entry points to prevent re-entry
- Clean and sanitize affected areas
- Repair any damage

### Step 4: Prevent Future Intrusions

After removal, it's critical to seal all potential entry points. A thorough exclusion includes:

- Repairing damaged soffits and fascia
- Installing chimney caps
- Screening attic vents
- Sealing gaps around roof lines
- Trimming tree branches away from the roof

## Legal Considerations

In Pennsylvania, many wildlife species are protected by state and federal laws. Bats, in particular, have specific regulations about when and how they can be excluded. Working with a licensed wildlife control operator ensures legal compliance.
